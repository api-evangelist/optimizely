<p>For most of marketing history, the most dastardly of bottlenecks was content generation or production. You had an idea, but turning it into something &mdash; the copy, the creative, the campaign &mdash; took time, budget, and headcount. Production scarcity shaped the whole profession.</p>
<p>That constraint is gone.</p>
<p style="text-align: left;">At <a href="https://www.optimizely.com/insights/agents-in-action/">Agents in Action</a>, Optimizely's President, Shafqat Islam sat down with Nathaniel Whittemore, host of <em>The AI Daily Brief</em>, to dig into what that <em>actually</em> means for marketers. When generation becomes infinite, what gets scarce? What gets valuable? And what should marketers be doing differently right now?</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"></p>
<h2>Agents for marketing X <em>Dr Strange</em> (don't ask, just read)</h2>
<p>Whittemore's answer to the infinite production question wasn't "human creativity" as a vague platitude &mdash; it was something more specific.</p>
<p>"We're not going to have one agent that writes copy," he said. "We're going to have 130 of them. Thirty are going to be in the voice of previous campaigns you've run. Thirty are going to be in the voice of competitors' campaigns. Forty are going to be in the voices of famous authors. And you're going to see what works best."</p>
<p>The reference he reached for was Dr. Strange in <em>Infinity War</em>, scanning 14 million possible futures to find the one where the Avengers win. AI-powered marketing, Whittemore argued, is moving toward that same logic...</p>
<h4>...massive parallel generation, testing at a scale that was previously unthinkable, and letting performance data do the curation.</h4>
<p>A/B testing was already reshaping how marketers think. This is the logical endpoint; not two versions, but hundreds, running simultaneously, with agents doing the analysis.</p>
<h2><em>The new bottleneck</em>: Systems, not ideas</h2>
<p>Here's the catch. Infinite generation doesn't automatically mean infinite output, because the real bottleneck has shifted.</p>
<blockquote>
<h4><em>"Spinning up a huge amount of content is different than getting it into a pipeline where it can easily flow all the way to the endpoint,</em>" <strong>Whittemore said. </strong><em>"And right now, that's still nascent."</em></h4>
</blockquote>
<p>He used himself as an example: he produces at least 20 minutes of video content every day, six days a week &mdash; and does almost nothing with it on social, because he hasn't built the pipeline to make use of it.</p>
<p>For marketing teams, this is the real challenge of 2026. The generation problem is largely solved. The workflow and orchestration problem is not. The organizations that figure out how to move from idea &rarr; creation &rarr; options &rarr; selection &rarr; production &rarr; optimization (with agents handling the throughput at every stage) are the ones that will pull ahead.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>It's call <a href="https://www.optimizely.com/insights/blog/agent-orchestration-for-marketers/">agent orchestration</a> sweetie, look it up.</p>
</blockquote>
<h2><em>What AI can't know</em>: Context, timing, and the emotional aftertaste</h2>
<p>Whittemore made an observation that stopped the conversation cold: marketing may be uniquely exposed to AI's biggest blind spot.</p>
<blockquote>
<h4><em>"The training set of AI has more opportunity to fail in forward-looking marketing than in some other domains,"</em> he said, <em>"because marketing is a conversation that's happening in the moment &mdash; an accumulation of everything else people are experiencing."</em></h4>
</blockquote>
<p>He put it bluntly: drop a positive, happy ad the day after September 11th, and it doesn't matter how that looks. That context is everything, and it's the kind of thing AI doesn't inherently know.</p>
<p>The best definition of brand he'd ever heard came from internet pioneer Ze Frank: "Brand is the emotional aftertaste of an experience." And that, Whittemore argued, is something an LLM fundamentally cannot capture.</p>
<blockquote>
<h4><em>"LLMs do not have feelings. They can read everyone else's feelings to the extent that people report them &mdash; but mostly we feel our feelings. We don't talk about them."</em></h4>
</blockquote>
<p>This isn't an argument against AI in marketing. It's an argument for what humans in marketing are actually for. The marketers who will matter most aren't the ones who can generate the most content &mdash; it's the ones who have the intuition to know what should be made <em>right now</em>, for <em>this audience</em>, in <em>this moment</em>, and the courage to commit to it.</p>
<h2><em>Taste + courage</em>: The two things that don't scale</h2>
<p>Islam put it simply: "There's taste and then there's courage. When you bring those two things together, you curate really special stuff."</p>
<p>That combination &mdash; genuine, creative judgment paired with the willingness to make a call &mdash; doesn't show up in a prompt. It comes from people who know the brand deeply, understand the cultural moment, and have strong enough convictions to back something unusual when the data doesn't have an answer yet.</p>
<p>AI can generate the options. It can surface the patterns. It can run the tests. <strong>But someone still has to decide what's worth making in the first place.</strong></p>
<h2>The <em>acceleration</em> <em>gap</em> is oh-so-very real</h2>
<p>One final thread from the conversation worth sitting with: the people and organizations that are actively using these tools well are not just ahead &mdash; they're compounding. The gap between early adopters and everyone else isn't holding steady. It's widening.</p>
<p>Whittemore was blunt about this. "The people who are using it well are rocketing to the moon." And the window to close that gap isn't getting wider.</p>
<h4>For marketers, the opportunity isn't to use AI to do what you already do, slightly faster. It's to rethink the system entirely &mdash; the workflows, the pipelines, the way judgment and automation interact &mdash; and figure out where human taste and courage create value that generation alone never will.</h4>
<p>That's the question worth asking right now. Not "how do we use AI to make more content?" but "now that we can make anything, what should we actually make?"</p>
